ABOUT


Alan Mendez is a New York City-based software engineer, data scientist, and creative problem solver. Prior to his work in the tech industry, Alan spent four years working as an actor, musician, and composer, where he gained firsthand experience cultivating his skills in collaboration and product development. Alan went on to gain certification in Machine Learning by Stanford University via Coursera, exploring applications ranging from product recommendation systems to statistical modeling methods including linear regression and neural networks. Most recently, Alan was contracted as a web developer with Jen Waldman Studio: Online, where he built and implemented customized HTML and CSS blocks in a beta software built in Bootstrap.

In his free time, Alan enjoys reading and feeding his love of learning. His main side project includes building a mobile web app that connects New York City artists with local rehearsal studio spaces based on real-time availability data, to acquaint arts workers with often overlooked venues and connect studio spaces with additional potential customers. Alan also has extensive knowledge of French and American wines, which he enjoys with cheese of any nationality.

  • Book-It

    Ever needed to find a nearby rehearsal studio space to prepare before your last-minute audition? Find yourself with some downtime between open calls, and want to see which venues have what you're really looking for? Or need to find another place to teach your music lesson on short notice? Book-It connects artists with local rehearsal studios in the New York City area, providing users with real-time room availability and pricing so that they can spend less time stressing, and more time focusing on what really matters. Find it. Book it.

    Wireframed in Swift, the next steps of this project are to build out and maintain a database to manage client-user information and implement a recommendation system to help users find spaces that meet their location and budget needs.

  • Raspberry Pi Timing Resolution

    In the world of nuclear and particle physics, the muon is a crucial subatomic particle. But it doesn't stay around very long--it's average lifetime is about 2 microseconds. How can such an important yet elusive particle be studied in a cost-effective manner? This project presents the research conducted as part of a data-taking team of physicists to test the timing resolution of a Raspberry Pi and determine its suitability as an operating system for a low-cost muon detector.
